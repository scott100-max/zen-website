Resilience

Learned Helplessness — and How to Unlearn It

7 min read · Published February 2026

Seligman's landmark research showed that when people believe they have no control, they stop trying — even when control returns. Recognising the pattern is the first step out. This isn't about positive thinking. It's about understanding how the brain learns to give up, and what the research says about teaching it to try again.

The Discovery

In 1967, Martin Seligman was a graduate student at the University of Pennsylvania when he stumbled onto something that would reshape the field of psychology. Working in a learning laboratory, he observed an unexpected phenomenon: animals that had been exposed to inescapable negative stimuli — situations where nothing they did made any difference — subsequently failed to take action even when escape became possible. They had learned, through repeated experience, that their behaviour didn't matter. So they stopped behaving.

Martin Seligman and Steven Maier termed this phenomenon "learned helplessness." Their research, published in the Journal of Experimental Psychology, demonstrated that the critical variable was not the negative experience itself, but the perceived absence of control. When subjects learned that their actions had no effect on outcomes, they generalised that belief to new situations — even ones where action would have been effective. The helplessness wasn't about the situation. It was about the belief.

The implications were immediate and profound. Seligman recognised that the same pattern appeared in humans — not in laboratories, but in homes, schools, workplaces, and relationships. People who had repeatedly experienced situations where their efforts made no difference developed a characteristic passivity: a quiet, pervasive sense that trying was pointless. Not laziness. Not apathy. A learned conviction that action and outcome were disconnected.

How It Looks in Real Life

Learned helplessness rarely announces itself. It doesn't feel like a psychological phenomenon — it feels like reality. It sounds like common sense. It passes for self-knowledge. That's what makes it so difficult to recognise from the inside.

"There's no point applying — I won't get it."
A single past rejection has been generalised into a universal prediction. The brain has concluded that effort and outcome are unrelated, so effort is withdrawn.
"This is just who I am. I've always been like this."
A temporary pattern has been cemented into identity. Change has been ruled out not through evidence, but through a belief that the self is fixed.
"It doesn't matter what I do — the result will be the same."
The hallmark statement of learned helplessness. The belief that action is decoupled from outcome, making effort feel irrational.
"Other people can do that, but I can't."
Success is attributed to something inherent in others that the speaker lacks — talent, luck, confidence. The possibility that skills can be developed is dismissed before it's tested.

The language is important because it reveals the underlying structure. Learned helplessness operates through three dimensions that Seligman later identified in his "explanatory style" framework: permanence (this will never change), pervasiveness (this affects everything), and personalisation (this is my fault, or my limitation). When all three converge, the result is a worldview in which effort feels futile.

The Three Ps

Seligman, working with colleagues Lyn Abramson and John Teasdale, developed the "attributional reformulation" of learned helplessness — a model explaining why some people develop helplessness after setbacks while others don't. The key variable is explanatory style: how people habitually explain the causes of negative events. A pessimistic explanatory style interprets setbacks as permanent ("this will always be the case"), pervasive ("this undermines everything"), and personal ("this is about me"). An optimistic style treats them as temporary, specific, and external.
Key Concept

The same event — a failed job interview, for instance — produces radically different outcomes depending on explanatory style. "I'm not good enough" (permanent, pervasive, personal) leads to withdrawal and avoidance. "That particular interview didn't go well because I was underprepared for that specific format" (temporary, specific, external) leads to adjustment and another attempt. The event is identical. The explanation determines the response.

Crucially, explanatory style is not a personality trait set in stone. It's a habit of thought — one that was learned and can therefore be unlearned. This was, in fact, the insight that led Seligman from learned helplessness to what would become his most famous contribution: the field of positive psychology.

The Revision

In 2016, Seligman and Maier published a significant revision to their original theory. After nearly fifty years and hundreds of studies, they concluded that they had the mechanism backwards. Helplessness is not learned — it is the brain's default response to uncontrollable events. What is learned is the expectation that action will work.

Maier and Seligman (2016) published their revised model in Psychological Review. They demonstrated that the default neural response to adversity is passivity — mediated by serotonin circuits in the dorsal raphe nucleus. Active coping requires the prefrontal cortex to detect control and override the default passive response. When the prefrontal cortex has learned that action leads to outcomes, it inhibits the passivity circuit. When it hasn't — or when it's been overwhelmed — the default remains.

This revision has a hopeful implication. If helplessness is the default and control is learned, then the antidote is not willpower or positive thinking — it's experience. Specifically, repeated experiences of agency: situations where action reliably produces outcomes. Each successful instance of "I did something and it mattered" strengthens the prefrontal circuitry that overrides the passivity default.

You don't unlearn helplessness by thinking differently. You unlearn it by doing something — however small — and experiencing the fact that it worked.

Unlearning the Pattern

The research points to several mechanisms through which learned helplessness can be reversed. None of them require dramatic transformation. All of them start small.

The first is what Seligman calls "learned optimism" — the deliberate practice of challenging pessimistic explanations and generating alternative ones. This is not about denial or forced positivity. It's a structured cognitive exercise: when you notice yourself explaining a setback as permanent, pervasive, and personal, you pause and look for evidence of temporariness, specificity, and external factors. Over time, this shifts the explanatory style from automatic pessimism to something more flexible and accurate.

Carol Dweck, professor of psychology at Stanford University, contributed a complementary framework through her research on mindset. Dweck demonstrated that people who believe their abilities are fixed ("fixed mindset") respond to failure with helplessness — avoidance, withdrawal, reduced effort. Those who believe abilities can be developed ("growth mindset") respond to failure with increased effort and strategy adjustment. The belief about whether change is possible directly determines whether change is attempted.

The second mechanism is behavioural activation — a core component of cognitive behavioural therapy for depression, which shares significant overlap with learned helplessness. Behavioural activation works by breaking the cycle of withdrawal: instead of waiting to feel better before acting, you act first and allow the experience of agency to rebuild the connection between effort and outcome. The actions don't need to be large. They need to be completed.

The third — and perhaps most powerful — is what the revised Seligman and Maier model implies: creating conditions where control is real and detectable. Environments that offer genuine choice, where effort visibly connects to results, and where failure is treated as information rather than identity, systematically rebuild the prefrontal circuitry that overrides helplessness. This has implications not just for individuals but for schools, workplaces, and relationships.

Something to Try This Week
The Agency Exercise
  1. Identify one area where you feel stuck. Where do you notice the "there's no point" reflex? It might be career, health, a relationship, a creative project, or something smaller. Pick the one that feels most familiar.
  2. Listen to the explanation. What's the story you tell yourself about why this situation won't change? Write it down. Then ask: is this permanent, or could it be temporary? Is it pervasive (affects everything), or specific to this situation? Is it personal (about who I am), or are there external factors?
  3. Find the smallest possible action. Not the action that fixes the problem — the action that proves you can act. Send one email. Make one phone call. Write one paragraph. Walk for ten minutes. The size doesn't matter. The agency does.
  4. Notice the result. Not whether the outcome was what you wanted, but whether your action produced any change at all. Even a small response — a reply, a shift in how you feel, a new piece of information — is evidence that action and outcome are connected. That evidence is the antidote.
  5. Repeat tomorrow. One action per day for a week. Each completed action strengthens the neural pathway that says "what I do matters." The helplessness wasn't built in a day. The agency won't be either. But it starts with the first deliberate step.
