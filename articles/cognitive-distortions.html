Cognitive Distortions: The Lies Your Brain Tells on Repeat

7 min read · Published February 2026

All-or-nothing thinking, catastrophising, mind reading — your brain has a shortlist of distortions it reaches for under stress. They feel like accurate assessments of reality. They're not. They're mental shortcuts that evolved to keep you alive, not to keep you accurate. Learning to spot them is half the battle.

The Brain's Built-In Bias

Your brain is not a neutral observer. It never was. Every perception you have is filtered through layers of assumption, expectation, and prior experience — most of which operate beneath conscious awareness. This is not a flaw. For most of human history, rapid pattern-matching kept us alive. The cost of a false alarm — mistaking a shadow for a predator — was low. The cost of missing a real threat was death. So the brain evolved to err on the side of caution, which means it evolved to err.

Aaron T. Beck, professor of psychiatry at the University of Pennsylvania and the founder of cognitive therapy, was the first to formally describe these patterns. In his 1976 work Cognitive Therapy and the Emotional Disorders, Beck identified a set of systematic errors in thinking — consistent, predictable ways that the mind distorts incoming information. He called them cognitive distortions, and he argued that they were not random glitches but structured habits of thought that maintain depression and anxiety.

Beck's insight was that these errors are not occasional lapses in logic. They are default settings. Under stress, fatigue, or emotional pain, the brain falls back on them automatically — like a computer reverting to factory presets. David Burns, a student of Beck's, later popularised these patterns in his 1980 bestseller Feeling Good, giving them the accessible names most people recognise today. But the underlying research is rigorous, clinical, and decades deep.

The Most Common Distortions

There are at least a dozen well-documented cognitive distortions. The following six are the ones that show up most frequently in clinical settings — and in everyday life. You will almost certainly recognise yourself in at least three of them.

All-or-Nothing Thinking
Seeing things in only two categories — perfect or worthless, success or failure — with no middle ground. One mistake becomes total defeat.
"I ate a biscuit so the whole diet is ruined. Might as well finish the packet."
Catastrophising
Jumping to the worst possible outcome and treating it as the most likely one. A small setback becomes evidence of impending disaster.
"My manager wants to talk to me. I'm probably getting fired. I'll lose the house."
Mind Reading
Assuming you know what other people are thinking — usually that they're judging you negatively — without any actual evidence.
"Everyone at the dinner could tell I was anxious. They think I'm pathetic."
Emotional Reasoning
Treating feelings as facts. Because something feels true, it must be true — regardless of objective evidence to the contrary.
"I feel like a failure, so I must be one."
Personalisation
Taking excessive responsibility for events that are not entirely — or even partially — within your control. Blaming yourself for other people's behaviour or external circumstances.
"My friend cancelled plans. I must have done something to upset her."
Discounting the Positive
Dismissing positive experiences, achievements, or feedback as irrelevant, fluky, or not counting. Good things happened, but they don't matter.
"They only said my presentation was good because they felt sorry for me."

Why Knowing Isn't Enough

You might read the list above and think: I know I do this. I can see it clearly. And that recognition is genuinely useful — it's the necessary first step. But recognition alone rarely changes the pattern. Cognitive distortions persist not because we lack insight, but because they operate at the speed of reflex. The distorted thought arrives fully formed, already feeling true, before you've had a chance to evaluate it.

Daniel Kahneman, Nobel laureate in economics and professor emeritus at Princeton, describes this as the difference between System 1 and System 2 thinking. In Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011), Kahneman explains that System 1 — fast, automatic, intuitive — generates impressions and feelings without effort or intention. System 2 — slow, deliberate, analytical — is what we think of as reasoning. The problem: System 1 runs the show most of the time, and cognitive distortions are System 1 products.

This is why distortions feel so real. They arrive with the authority of perception, not the tentativeness of hypothesis. When your brain tells you "everyone noticed your mistake," it doesn't present that as a guess. It presents it as a fact you observed. You feel the social exposure. You feel the judgement. And because emotional reasoning is itself one of the distortions, the feeling becomes its own evidence.

"The goal is not to eliminate distortions — that would require a different brain. The goal is to create a gap between the thought arriving and you believing it."

Creating the Gap

If distortions are automatic thoughts — and they are — then the intervention point is not prevention. You cannot stop them from arising. What you can do is learn to notice them after they arrive and before they dictate your behaviour. This is the shared territory of cognitive behavioural therapy and mindfulness-based approaches: both aim to insert a pause between stimulus and response, between the thought arriving and you acting on it as though it were true.

Zindel Segal, professor of psychology at the University of Toronto, along with Mark Williams and John Teasdale, developed Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT) specifically to address this problem. MBCT combines the cognitive awareness of CBT with the present-moment attention training of mindfulness meditation. Rather than challenging distorted thoughts directly, MBCT teaches patients to observe thoughts as mental events — passing phenomena that can be noticed without being believed.

The results have been substantial. Teasdale, Segal, Williams, and colleagues (2000) demonstrated that MBCT reduced relapse rates in recurrent depression by nearly 50% compared to treatment as usual. The mechanism was not that patients learned to think more positively. It was that they learned to relate to their thoughts differently — to see them as weather rather than truth.

This shift — from being inside a thought to observing it — is sometimes called metacognition: thinking about thinking. It sounds abstract, but the practical effect is concrete. When you can say "there's the catastrophising again" instead of "I'm going to lose everything," the distortion loses its grip. Not because it disappears, but because you've created enough distance to choose whether to follow it.

Research Insight

A 2019 meta-analysis by Faissner, Kriston, Moritz, and Jelinek, published in Clinical Psychology Review, examined the relationship between cognitive distortions and psychological distress across 45 studies. They found a consistent, moderate-to-strong association between the frequency of distorted thinking patterns and symptoms of depression, anxiety, and psychosis — and that interventions targeting these distortions produced significant symptom reduction across diagnostic categories.

Something to Try Today

The Thought Record
1 When you notice a shift in mood — anxiety rising, irritation spiking, sadness descending — pause and write down the thought that preceded it. Not the situation. The thought. Be as specific as possible.
2 Name the distortion. Look at the thought and ask: is this all-or-nothing? Am I catastrophising? Am I mind reading? You don't need to get it exactly right. The act of labelling is what matters.
3 Ask yourself: what evidence do I actually have for this thought? Not what it feels like. What would hold up if someone else examined the facts?
4 Write an alternative thought — not a positive affirmation, but a more balanced version. "Everyone thinks I'm stupid" might become "I made one mistake in a meeting. Most people probably weren't paying close attention."
5 Notice how the balanced thought feels compared to the original. You don't need to believe it fully. Just notice whether the emotional intensity shifts, even slightly. That shift is the gap widening.
